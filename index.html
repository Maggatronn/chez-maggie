Maggie Hughes.

Civic technologist, organizer, and product lead. PhD candidate at MIT Media Lab, advised by Deb Roy.

Currently

Co-founder of Convoca — building V2V and LumoViz with city planners, grassroots federations, and research labs.

Most civic technology is built for transactional engagement — broadcast, extract, summarize, move on. I build for sustained relationship: systems that make the relational infrastructure of civic life legible, so engagement compounds into capacity and power rather than dissipating after each interaction.

Microsoft Sociotechnical Systems PhD Fellow. Metagov 2025 Interoperability grant. MIT Prize for Open Data, Honorable Mention. Affiliated with Harvard's Practicing Democracy Project (with Marshall Ganz) and MIT's Data + Feminism Lab.

Civic decision-making platform

Voice to Vision

A production sociotechnical platform that translates community input into structured infrastructure for neighborhood planning. Multilingual story submission, a public map of resident contributions, and a transparent "vision" layer that shows community members how their input is being used.

Deployed with NYC Department of City Planning — Jamaica (2024) · White Plains Road (2025)

Paper · Jamaica · White Plains Road

Relational organizing data platform

LumoViz

Production data infrastructure for grassroots organizing federations — relational mapping, conversation tracking, and team-based campaigning. Developed with the Carolina Federation through participatory design over three years; supports 20+ practitioners across North Carolina.

CSCW 2021 · GROUP 2027 (under review)

Measurement tools for civic dialogue

Conversation Dynamics

An open analysis environment for facilitated conversations — speaker timelines, thread depth, responsivity metrics, and turn-taking networks. Used by researchers and practitioners studying constructive communication in deliberative settings.

EMNLP 2025 · DIS 2024 · Demo

Facilitated conversation environment

Keeper

A synchronous online conversation environment informed by in-person facilitation practices — turn-taking queue, shared guidelines, hand-raising, and consent built into the room itself. The foundational tool behind this line of work.
